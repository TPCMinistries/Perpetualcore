## Section 2 — The eight registries
EYEBROW: The substrate.
H2: Eight registries. One operating model.

[VISUAL: 8-node registry diagram — labeled nodes with thin connecting lines, monochrome primary-tinted, no gradients. This is the canonical visual asset for the studio site, promoted from the homepage hero per UI audit §2.]

BODY (one short paragraph per registry, 1–2 sentences each):

**Entities.** Every organization, fund, partner, vendor, and counterparty your operation touches. Resolved, deduplicated, and queryable. The first thing your team stops re-typing into spreadsheets.

**People.** Staff, beneficiaries, volunteers, board members, students, members. Roles, relationships, consent state, and audit trail. Every person in your org has one record, not seventeen.

**Projects.** Programs, initiatives, grants, contracts, engagements — the work containers that hold budget, scope, and accountability. Tied to the entities funding them and the people doing them.

**Work items.** Tasks, deliverables, follow-ups. The thing your team thought was in Asana, Notion, three Slack channels, and a voice note from last Tuesday. Now in one registry, with provenance.

**Knowledge.** Documents, voice notes, calls, channels, embeddings. Synthesized into one queryable mind. This is the registry Vellum operates on directly — see /products/vellum.

**Agents.** The AI workers your team has built and authorized. Each with a defined scope, audit log, and refusal rules. Not "AI" as a vibe — agents as a registered, accountable category of operator.

**Workflows.** Automated sequences across registries. Built in the AI-First Framework's Automate phase. Versioned, modifiable, owned by your team after handover.

**Events.** Every state change, every approval, every refusal. The audit log that lets you answer "who did what when" six months later, in front of a regulator if it comes to that.

## Section 3 — The AI-First Framework
EYEBROW: The methodology.
H2: Learn → Wire → Automate → Scale.

BODY:
Four phases. None skipped.

**Learn.** Two weeks of operator-grade reading. Calls, docs, voice notes, channels. We don't ask you to fill out an intake form. We sit in the meetings.

**Wire.** Three to four weeks. The eight registries get installed in your stack — your Supabase, your storage, your auth. Your operators are querying live data by week 5.

**Automate.** Six to ten weeks. Skills get built against your real workflows. Anthropic SKILL.md format, per-portco JSON. Versioned, auditable, owned by you.

**Scale.** Two to four weeks. Your team operates and extends the system. We document. We train. We hand over.

This is the same arc on every engagement, scaled to the band. The four phases don't shift — what shifts is how many departments and how many skills.

## Section 4 — The skills library
EYEBROW: The compounding asset.
H2: Skills, in the Anthropic SKILL.md format.

BODY:
Every skill we build is a versioned, auditable unit of automated work. Written in the Anthropic SKILL.md format — markdown frontmatter, prose body, optional code blocks — with a per-portco JSON config that scopes it to your operating context.

Your operators can read every skill. Audit it. Modify it. Add to it. There are no black boxes in your skills library, because the moment a black box appears, your team stops trusting the system, and the system stops being yours.

By the time you finish an Operations engagement, you have 15 to 30 production skills. By the time you finish an Institutional engagement, you have a library that outlives the engagement.

This is the compounding part. Every engagement we run adds skills back to the library. Your operators inherit work we did at other organizations under the same constraint regime — pre-vetted, pre-tested, ready to adapt.

## Section 5 — The Engine commitment
EYEBROW: The mission.
H2: 10% of every engagement — $7,500 to $25,000+ per client — funds the Institute for Human Advancement.

BODY:
The Institute for Human Advancement is our 501(c)(3) parent. It runs workforce development for low-income New Yorkers, healthcare-pathway training in community-college workforce programs, and field health work in East Africa. The studio exists to fund the mission — not the other way around.

Here's what flows where:

| Surface | % to the Institute for Human Advancement |
|---|---|
| Engagements | 10% ($7,500–$25,000+ per client) |
| Sage SaaS | 15% (per Sage PRD v0.3) |
| All other products and Platform | 10% default |

The mixed rate is intentional. Sage carries 15% because Sage is positioned as an owned-perpetual-asset for individual operators, and the higher rate is part of its differentiation. Engagements stay at 10% to keep the math defensible at scale. Either way, the floor is non-negotiable.

This is structurally non-replicable.

No VC-backed competitor can give away 10 to 15% of top-line revenue. Their cap table won't allow it. No JV can either; their LPs won't either. The Anthropic-Blackstone venture, the OpenAI-TPG venture, EPAM's 250 Black Belts, every Big-4 AI practice — none of them can do this. It is the one moat we have that does not depend on technology.

It is also why a foundation program officer, a faith-institution executive director, or a UN-agency operations director can put us through procurement without explaining to their board why they hired a vendor whose entire margin compounds inside the same investor class they're trying to escape.

The Institute for Human Advancement is audited annually. The contribution is line-itemed on every invoice. We don't bury this in a footer.
